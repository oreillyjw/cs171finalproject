About Disability-Adjusted Life Years (DALY)

As the years tick by, the majority of people find themselves affected by disability, disease, or injury. The time spent living with these burdens combined with time of life lost come together to form a metric called Disability Adjusted Life Year (DALY). This measure was formed in the 1990s as a mechanism to easily draw comparisons on a country-by-country basis. Now, with all of the information available, it can be sliced and diced in a variety of ways to observe trends and gain actionable insights.

From birth until life-impacting disease is considered “healthy life.” It is acknowledged that throughout this healthy period, there will be some brief stints of illness, but nothing severely debilitating. This is a part of DALY, but it is not counted in the calculation.
After disability or disease take their toll, the next phase of life is considered, “Years Lived with Disability” (YLD). Disability goes beyond traditional views; rather, in the context of DALYs, it can be any disease, illness, disability, or injury that reduces a person’s quality of life. It is a life event that severely impacts and changes not only the day-to-day, but can change their life expectancy.
The final piece of the DALY metric is “years of life lost” (YLL). Early death is an unfortunate result of YLD, and the years lost are what is taken from what should have been the person’s true life expectancy. YLD + YLL = DALY.
